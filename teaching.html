Section 05

Planning & Teaching

Planning

Each Nova school has autonomy over the frequency and duration they spend on history each year. For example, history may only be taught once but in greater depth at one school, in comparison to two or three smaller units at another school. All schools create medium term plans which identify the disciplinary and substantive knowledge children need to achieve within each lesson and unit. This ensures there is progression within and across units in each year group/phase. Once these have been identified, disciplinary concepts, such as continuity and change, are identified and are referred to into the lessons.

Use of Sources

Sources serve as the fundamental building blocks of historical enquiry. They provide the hinterland detail that makes core knowledge memorable and ‘sticky’ and support chronological awareness through dated artefacts and images. Pupils learn that historians use sources to construct claims, arguments, and accounts, and that any historical judgement must be underpinned by rigorous evidence.

Our curriculum ensures that pupils encounter a diverse range of sources within a rich framework of substantive knowledge. Each source that’s been selected has a clear purpose, has guided, rather than open ended questions, and adults model how historians would interpret them. As a result, sources are integral to the majority of our lessons and allow pupils to refine their questioning skills and develop curiosity.

Supporting the English Curriculum

Wherever possible, each Nova school makes links between the history and English curriculum. Examples include:

Additional reading sessions

Children independently read texts linking to the history unit. Where appropriate, children respond to comprehension questions surrounding this.

Vocabulary

Within each unit, key vocabulary is identified. This is explicitly taught, with children incorporating it into their discussions and written work.

Structuring a written response to a source

When extended written responses are required, scaffolding is provided to ensure children structure their paragraph accurately. (See Writing playbook.)

Written assessments

A written end of unit assessment is planned in the same way as an extended piece of writing in English. Children refer to success criteria, plan, draft and publish their work. This assessment can take part during English or history lessons.

Adaptation

Our history curriculum is designed to be accessible and ambitious for all learners. Pupils with SEND are supported through explicit instruction, structured scaffolding, adaptive teaching and flexible assessment methods, ensuring meaningful engagement with both substantive knowledge and disciplinary thinking. Examples of adaptations include oral rehearsal, visual prompts, chunking, sentence starters or targeted questions.
